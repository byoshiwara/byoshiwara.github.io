In ExampleÂ 3.77 we saw that large animals eat less than smaller ones, relative to their body weight. This is because the scaling exponent in Kleiber's rule is less than \(1\text{.}\) For example, let \(s\) represent the mass of a squirrel. The mass of a moose is then \(600s\text{,}\) and its metabolic rate is \begin{align*} B(600s) \amp = 70(600s)^{0.75} \\ \amp = 600^{0.75} \cdot 70s^{0.75} = 121B(s) \end{align*} or \(121\) times the metabolic rate of the squirrel. Metabolic rate scales as \(k^{0.75}\text{,}\) compared to the mass of the animal.
