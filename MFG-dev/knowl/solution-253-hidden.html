1. Because \(L\) varies as the fourth power of \(M\text{,}\) we have

    \begin{equation*} L = kM^4 \end{equation*}
    Substituting the values of \(L\) and \(M\) for the Sun (namely, \(L = 1\) and \(M = 1\)), we find
    \begin{equation*} 1 = k(1)^4 \end{equation*}
    so \(k = 1\) and \(L = M^4\text{.}\)

  2. We substitute the luminosity of Sirius, \(L = 23\text{,}\) to get

    \begin{equation*} 23 = M^4 \end{equation*}
    To solve the equation for \(M\text{,}\) we raise both sides to the \(\dfrac{1}{4}\) power.
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}[t] (23)^{1/4} \amp = \left(M^4\right)^{1/4} \\ 2.1899 \amp = M \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    The mass of Sirius is about \(2.2\) solar masses, or about \(2.2\) times the mass of the Sun.
