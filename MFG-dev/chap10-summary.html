Section 10.6 Chapter Summary and Review

Subsection Key Concepts

  1. The most general form of a second-degree equation in two variables is
    \begin{equation*} Ax^2 + Bxy + Cy^2 + Dx + Ey + F = 0 \end{equation*}
    where \(A, B\text{,}\) and \(C\) cannot all be zero, and its graph is a conic section.
  2. An ellipse is the set of points in the plane the sum of whose distances from two fixed points (called the foci) is a constant.
  3. The standard form for an ellipse centered at the origin is
    \begin{equation*} \dfrac{x^2}{a^2} + \dfrac{y^2}{b^2} = 1 \end{equation*}
  4. A hyperbola is the set of points in the plane, the difference of whose distances from two fixed points (the foci) is a constant.
  5. The standard form for a hyperbola centered at the origin with foci on the \(x\)-axis is
    \begin{equation*} \dfrac{x^2}{a^2} - \dfrac{y^2}{b^2} = 1 \end{equation*}
    The standard form for a hyperbola centered at the origin with foci on the \(y\)-axis is
    \begin{equation*} \dfrac{y^2}{b^2} - \dfrac{x^2}{a^2} = 1 \end{equation*}
  6. We can draw the asymptotes by first forming the "central rectangle," whose sides pass through the vertices and covertices. The asymptotes are the diagonals of this rectangle.
  7. A parabola is the set of points in the plane whose distances from a fixed line \(l\) and a fixed point \(F\) are equal.
  8. Parabolas with vertex at the origin have equations of the form
    \begin{equation*} y=\dfrac{x^2}{4p} ~~~~~~ \text{or} ~~~~~~ y=\dfrac{-x^2}{4p} ~~~~~~ \text{or} ~~~~~~ x=\dfrac{y^2}{4p} ~~~~~~ \text{and} ~~~~~~ x=\dfrac{-y^2}{4p} \end{equation*}
  9. The standard equation for an ellipse centered at \((h,k)\) is
    \begin{equation*} \dfrac{(x-h)^2}{a^2} + \dfrac{(y-k)^2}{b^2} = 1 \end{equation*}
  10. Second-degree equations can be written in one of the standard forms by completing the square.
  11. To write the equation of an ellipse from a description of its properties, we must find the center of the ellipse and the lengths of its axes.
  12. The standard equations for a hyperbola centered \((h,k)\) at are
    \begin{equation*} \dfrac{(x-h)^2}{a^2} - \dfrac{(y-k)^2}{b^2} = 1~~\text{and}~~\dfrac{(y-k)^2}{b^2} - \dfrac{(x-h)^2}{a^2} = 1 \end{equation*}
  13. The standard equation for a parabola with vertex at has one of the following forms.
    \begin{equation*} y-k=a(x-h)^2~~~~~\text{opens upward if}~a \gt 0;~~\text{downward if}~a \lt 0 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} x-h=a(y-k)^2~~~~~\text{opens to the right if}~a \gt 0;~~\text{to the left if}~a \lt 0 \end{equation*}
  14. The constant \(a\) is equal to \(\dfrac{\pm1}{4p}\text{,}\) where \(p\) is the distance between the vertex and the focus of the parabola.
  15. The coefficients \(D, E,\) and \(F\) do not affect the type of conic section the equation represents, but they do determine the position of the graph relative to the origin.
  16. The graph of a second-degree equation in two variables is a conic section.

  17. The graph of \(~Ax^2 + Bxy + Cy^2 + Dx + Ey + F = 0~\) is

    1. a circle if \(A=C\text{.}\)
    2. a parabola if \(A=0\) or \(C=0\) (but not both).
    3. an ellipse if \(A \not= C\) and they have the same sign.
    4. a hyperbola if \(A\) and \(C\) have opposite signs.
  18. Systems involving conics may have up to four solutions.
  19. To solve some quadratic systems, we can use a combination of elimination of variables and substitution.
