    Section 7.7 Projects for Chapter 7

    Project 7.3. Solving cubics: Part I.

    In this project, we solve cubic equations of the form
    \begin{equation*} x^3 + mx = n \end{equation*}
    Note that there is no quadratic term. This special form was first solved by the Italian mathematicians Scipione del Ferro and Niccolò Fontana Tartaglia early in the sixteenth century. Tartaglia revealed the secret to solving the special cubic equation in a poem. He first found values \(u\) and \(v\) to satisfy the system
    \begin{gather*} u-v=n\\ uv=\left(\frac{m}{3}\right)^3 \end{gather*}
    1. We will use Tartaglia’s method to solve
      \begin{equation*} x^3 + 6x = 7 \end{equation*}
      What are the values of \(m\) and \(n\text{?}\)
    2. Substitute the values of \(m\) and \(n\) into Tartaglia’s system, then use substitution to solve for \(u\) and \(v\text{.}\) You should find two possible solutions.
    3. For each solution of the system, compute \(x=\sqrt[3]{u}- \sqrt[3]{v} \text{.}\) You should get the same value of \(x\) for each \((u, v)\text{.}\)
    4. Check that your value for \(x\) is a solution of \(x^3 + 6x = 7\text{.}\)

    Project 7.4. Solving cubics: Part II.

    Tartaglia’s method always works to solve the special cubic equation, even when \(u\) and \(v\) are not convenient values. We will show why in this project.
    1. Expand the expression \((a - b)^3 + 3ab(a - b)\) and complete the identity.
      \begin{equation*} (a-b)^3+3ab(a-b)=\fillinmath{XXXXXXXXXXXXXXX} \end{equation*}
    2. Your answer to part (a) is actually Tartaglia’s special cubic in disguise. Substitute \(x = a - b\text{,}\) \(m = 3ab\text{,}\) and \(n = a^3 - b^3\) to see this. Therefore, if we can find numbers \(a\) and \(b\) that satisfy
      \begin{align*} 3ab \amp = m\\ a^3 - b^3 \amp = n \end{align*}
      then the solution to Tartaglia’s cubic is \(x = a - b\text{.}\)
    3. Compare the system in part (b) to the system from Project 7.3,
      \begin{gather*} u - v = n\\ uv=\left(\frac{m}{3}\right)^3 \end{gather*}
      to show that \(u = a^3\) and \(v = b^3\text{.}\)
    4. Use your answer to part (c) to show that Tartaglia’s value, \(x=\sqrt[3]{u}- \sqrt[3]{v} \text{,}\) is a solution of \(x^3 + mx = n\text{.}\)

    Project 7.5. Solving cubics: Part III.

    Use Tartaglia’s method to solve the equation
    \begin{equation} x^3 + 3x = 2\tag{7.7.1} \end{equation}
    by carrying out the following steps.
    1. Identify the values of \(m\) and \(n\) from (7.7.1) and write two equations for \(u\) and \(v\text{.}\)
    2. Solve for values of \(u\) and \(v\text{.}\) You will need to use the quadratic formula.

    Project 7.8. Optimal traffic flow.

    The stopping distance, \(s\text{,}\) for a car traveling at speed \(v\) meters per second is given (in meters) by
    \begin{equation*} s=vT+\frac{v^2}{2a} \end{equation*}
    where \(T\) is the reaction time of the driver and \(a\) is the average deceleration as the car brakes. Suppose that all the cars on a crowded motorway maintain the appropriate spacing determined by the stopping distance for their speed.     \begin{equation*} t=\frac{s}{v}+\frac{L}{v} \end{equation*}
    where \(L\) is the length of the car. To achieve the maximum flow of cars, we would like \(t\) to be as small as possible. (Source: Bolton, 1974)

      \begin{align*} {\text{Xmin}} \amp = 0 \amp\amp {\text{Xmax}} = 20\\ {\text{Ymin}} \amp = 0 \amp\amp {\text{Ymax}} = 3 \end{align*}
    3. To one decimal place, what value of \(v\) gives the minimum value of \(t\text{?}\) Convert your answer to miles per hour.

    Project 7.10. Biological half-life.

    When a drug or chemical is injected into a patient, biological processes begin removing that substance. If no more of the substance in introduced, the body removes a fixed fraction of the substance each hour. The amount of substance remaining in the body at time \(t\) is an exponential decay function, so there is a biological half-life to the substance denoted by \(T_b\text{.}\) If the substance is a radioisotope, it undergoes radioactive decay and so has a physical half-life as well, denoted \(T_p\text{.}\)
    The effective half-life, denoted by \(T_e\text{,}\) is related to the biological and physical half-lives by the equation
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{T_e}=\frac{1}{T_b}+\frac{1}{T_p} \end{equation*}
    The radioisotope \(^{131}\text{I}\) is used as a label for the human serum albumin. The physical half-life of \(^{131}\text{I}\) is \(8\) days. (Source: Pope, 1989)
    1. If \(^{131}\text{I}\) is cleared from the body with a half-life of \(21\) days, what is the effective half-life of \(^{131}\text{I}\text{?}\)
    2. The biological half-life of a substance varies considerably from person to person. If the biological half-life of \(^{131}\text{I}\) is \(x\) days, what is the effective half life?
    3. Let \(f (x)\) represent the effective half-life of \(^{131}\text{I}\) when the biological half-life is \(x\) days. Graph \(y = f (x)\text{.}\)
    4. What would the biological half-life of \(^{131}\text{I}\) need to be to produce an effective half-life of \(6\) days? Label the corresponding point on your graph.
    5. For what possible biological half-lives of \(^{131}\text{I}\) will the effective half-life be less than \(4\) days?
