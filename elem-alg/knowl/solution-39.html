Solution 2.21.1.

  1. We choose \(x\)-values that are multiples of 3 and make a table of values.

    \(x\) \(y\) \(\hphantom{0000}\)
    \(-9\) \(2\) \(y=\dfrac{-2}{3}(\alert{-9})-4=2\)
    \(-6\) \(0\) \(y=\dfrac{-2}{3}(\alert{-6})-4=0\)
    \(0\) \(-4\) \(y=\dfrac{-2}{3}(\alert{0})-4=-4\)
    \(3\) \(-6\) \(y=\dfrac{-2}{3}(\alert{3})-4=-6\)
    \(6\) \(-8\) \(y=\dfrac{-2}{3}(\alert{6})-4=-8\)

    Then we plot the points and connect them with a straight line.

  2. We see that \(y\) has been replaced by \(-2\) in the equation of the graph. So we look for the point on the graph that has \(y\)-coordinate \(-2\text{.}\) This point, labeled \(P\) on the graph below, has \(x\)-coordinate \(-3\text{.}\)

    Because it lies on the graph, the point \(P(-3,-2)\) is a solution of the equation \(~y=\dfrac{-2}{3}x-4\text{.}\) When we substitute \(x=\blert{-3}\) and \(y=\alert{-2}\) into the equation, we get a true statement:

    \begin{equation*} \alert{-2}=\dfrac{-2}{3}(\blert{-3})-4 \end{equation*}

    But this statement also tells us that \(-3\) is a solution of the equation \(~\dfrac{-2}{3}x-4=-2\text{.}\)
