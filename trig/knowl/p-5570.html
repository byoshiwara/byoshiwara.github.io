Because \(\sec (\theta) = \dfrac{1}{\cos (\theta)}\text{,}\) we see that \(\dfrac{1}{\cos (\theta)}=3\text{,}\) or \(\cos (\theta) = \dfrac{1}{3}\text{.}\) We use the Pythagorean identity to find the sine.
\begin{align*} \cos^2 (\theta) + \sin^2 (\theta) \amp = 1 \amp \amp \blert{\text{Substitute}~ \frac{1}{3} \text{ for } \cos (\theta).}\\ \left(\dfrac{1}{3}\right)^2 + \sin^2 (\theta) \amp = 1 \amp \amp \blert{\text{Subtract}~ \left(\frac{1}{3}\right)^2=\frac{1}{9}~ \text{from both sides.}}\\ \sin^2 (\theta) \amp = 1 - \dfrac{1}{9}=\dfrac{8}{9} \end{align*}
Because \(\theta\) lies in the fourth quadrant, where the sine function is negative, we choose the negative square root for \(\sin (\theta)\text{.}\) Once we know \(\sin (\theta)\text{,}\) we calculate its reciprocal to find \(\csc(\theta)\text{.}\)
\begin{equation*} \sin (\theta) = -\sqrt{\dfrac{8}{9}} = \dfrac{-2\sqrt{2}}{3},~\text{ and }~\csc(\theta) = \dfrac{1}{\sin (\theta)} = \dfrac{-3}{2\sqrt{2}} = \dfrac{-3\sqrt{2}}{4} \end{equation*}
