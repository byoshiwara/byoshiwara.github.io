We use the polar form, \(z= 2(\cos \dfrac{\pi}{6} + i\sin \dfrac{\pi}{6})\text{,}\) and apply De Moivre's theorem. Then \begin{align*} z^4 \amp = 2^4(\cos 4 \cdot \dfrac{\pi}{6}+i\sin 4 \cdot \dfrac{\pi}{6})\\ \amp=16(\cos \dfrac{2\pi}{3} + i\sin \dfrac{2\pi}{3})\\ \end{align*} The Cartesian form of \(z^4\) is thus \(16(\dfrac{-1}{2} + i\dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2})\text{,}\) or \(-8+8i\sqrt{3}\text{.}\) You can check that you get this same result if you compute \((\sqrt{3} + i)^4\) by expanding the power.
