It is also easy to add vectors in coordinate form. The figure at right shows the sum of \({\bf{u}} = {\bf{i}}+2{\bf{j}}\) and \({\bf{v}} = 5{\bf{i}}+3{\bf{j}}\text{.}\) Remember that we add two vectors by following the first vector by the second. The vector sum describes the path

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}[t] 1~ \text{unit in the -direction, then}\\ 2~ \text{units in the -direction, then}\\ 5~ \text{units in the -direction, then}\\ 3~ \text{units in the -direction. }\\ \end{aligned} \end{equation*}

But we arrive at the same endpoint by traveling

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}[t] 1+5=6~ \text{units in the -direction, then}\\ 2+3=5~ \text{units in the -direction.}\\ \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
