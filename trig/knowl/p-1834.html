The vertices of a triangle determine a unique circumscribing circle.

  1. Draw a large acute triangle and label the vertices \(A\) , \(B\text{,}\) and \(C\text{.}\)

  2. Draw the perpendicular bisector of side \(\overline{AC}\text{.}\) You can use a compass, or use your ruler and protractor as follows:

    1. Find the midpoint, \(M\text{,}\) of side \(AC\text{.}\)

    2. Draw a line that passes through \(M\) and is perpendicular to side \(\overline{AC}\text{.}\)

  3. Draw the perpendicular bisector of side \(\overline{BC}\) . Label the point where the two perpendicular bisectors meet with the letter \(O\text{.}\)

  4. From geometry, we know that every point on the perpendicular bisector of a segment \(\overline{PQ}\) is equidistant between \(P\) and \(Q\text{.}\) Use this fact to explain why the point \(O\) is the same distance from each vertex of your triangle.

  5. Using the point at \(O\) as the center, draw the circle that passes through all three vertices of your triangle. The circle is called the circumscribing circle of the triangle.
