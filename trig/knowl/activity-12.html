Activity 8.2. Identities.

I Sum of Angles Identities

  1. Is it true that \(\cos (\theta + \phi) = \cos \theta + \cos \phi\text{?}\) Try it for \(\theta = 60\degree\) and \(\phi = 45\degree\text{.}\)
    1. Recall the distributive law, \(a(b+c) = ab + ac\text{,}\) where the parentheses denote multiplication. Is the same law true when the parentheses denote a function? In other words, is it true that \(f(a+b) = f(a) + f(b)\text{?}\)
    2. In the expression \(\sin (\theta + \phi)\text{,}\) do the parentheses denote multiplication or the application of a function? Does the distributive law apply to \(\sin (\theta + \phi)\text{?}\) Do you think that \(\sin (\theta + \phi) = \sin \theta + \sin \phi\) is an identity?
    1. Look at the Sum of Angles Identities in this section. Make some observations that will help you memorize these formulas.
    2. Do you think you would have to memorize these formulas if the equation \(\sin (\theta + \phi) = \sin \theta + \sin \phi\) were an identity?

II Difference of Angles Identities, Tangent Identities

    1. Yikes! More formulas. Compare the Difference of Angles Identities with the Sum of Angles Identities. If you have memorized the Sum formulas, how can you also memorize the Difference formulas?
    2. Comment on the sign patterns in the Sum and Difference Identities for Tangent.
    1. Now let's use the formulas backwards: look at the expression below:
      \begin{equation*} \dfrac{\tan 285\degree - \tan 75\degree}{1 + \tan 285\degree \tan 75\degree} \end{equation*}
      Does it remind you of the left side of one of the six new identities? Use that identity to simplify the expression.
    2. Do the same thing for this expression:
      \begin{equation*} \sin 4t \cos 0.7 - \cos 4t \sin 0.7 \end{equation*}

III Double Angle Identities

    1. Is it true that \(\sin 2\theta = 2\sin \theta\text{?}\) Choose a value for \(\theta\) and try it. Sketch \(\theta\) and \(2\theta\) on a unit circle and show the sine of each.
    2. Is it true that \(\cos 2\theta = 2\cos \theta\text{?}\) Repeat part (a) for cosine.
    1. Substitute \(\alpha = \theta\) and \(\beta = \theta\) into the sum of angles formulas for sine, cosine, and tangent to derive the double angle formulas.
    2. Choose one of the three forms of the Double Angle Identity for Cosine. Use the Pythagorean Identity to convert from that form to each of the other two forms.
