The unknown distance \(d\) is the side opposite \(\angle B = 79.4\degree\text{.}\) In order to use the Law of Sines, we must know another angle and the side opposite that angle. We do: we know that side \(c = 400\text{,}\) and we can compute the angle at the ship, \(\angle C\text{.}\)

\begin{equation*} \angle C = 180\degree - (79.4\degree + 83.2\degree) = 17.4\degree \end{equation*}

Now we apply the Law of Sines, using angles \(B\) and \(C\text{.}\)

\begin{align*} \dfrac{b}{\sin B} \amp = \dfrac{c}{\sin C} \amp\amp \blert{\text{Substitute the given values.}}\\ \dfrac{d}{\sin 79.4\degree} \amp = \dfrac{400}{\sin 17.4\degree} \amp\amp \blert{\text{Evaluate sines.}}\\ \dfrac{d}{0.9829} \amp = \dfrac{400}{0.2990} \amp\amp \blert{\text{Multiply both sides by 0.9829.}}\\ d \amp = 1315 \end{align*}

The ship is about 1315 yards from the observer at \(A\text{.}\)
