Caution 3.12.

In the previous example, you might notice that \(\tan \theta = \dfrac{-4}{3}\) and try to find by calculating \(\tan^{-1}\left(\dfrac{-4}{3}\right)\text{.}\) However, if we press

\(\qquad\qquad\qquad\)2nd TAN 4 รท 3 ) ENTER

the calculator returns an angle of \(\theta \approx -53.1 \degree\text{.}\) It is true that \(\tan (-53.1 \degree) = \dfrac{-4}{3}\text{,}\) but this is not the obtuse angle we want.

We also know that \(\sin \theta = \dfrac{4}{5}\text{,}\) and if we press

\(\qquad\qquad\quad\)2nd SIN 4 รท 5 ) ENTER

we get \(\theta \approx 53.1 \degree\text{.}\)

This is the acute angle whose terminal side passes through the point \((3,4)\text{,}\) as shown in the figure above. The angle we want is its supplement, \(\theta \approx 180\degree - 53.1\degree = 126.9\degree\text{.}\)
