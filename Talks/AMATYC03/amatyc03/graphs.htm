Creating Graphs for Handouts and Exams

The basic thing about graphs in Excel (as I draw them) is that they are all defined parametrically using the "XY (Scatter)" chart type. (Excel calls all graphs "charts.")

Samples

For some examples, open the file trig.xls in Excel. The sine and cosine graphs are easy and quick to make, but the tangent graph, which has five disconnected pieces, was created by plotting 5 separate curves (and simply formatting them with the same color and thickness). Formatting the x-axis to show multiples of pi is a clumsy trick discussed below.

To create a trig graph of your own, start by inserting a new sheet (Insert->Worksheet) or simply begin a new file. In the A1 cell type "-2" and Enter, then type "-1.9" and Enter. Select the two cells, click on the lower right corner of the selection and drag the black cross until you've filled in cells up to the number "2" in cell A41.

In B1 type

=sin(pi()*a1)

and Enter. Select B1 again and double-click on the lower right corner of the cell to fill in all the values of sin(pi*x) for x from -2 to 2.

Select the data you've created in the two columns and click on the chart icon (that looks like a bar graph with blue, yellow, and red bars). Under "Chart type:" select "XY (Scatter)." There are 5 "Chart sub-types" on the right; choose the smooth curves without markers. Click and hold on the "Press and Hold to View Sample" button, then click on Next twice.

Fill in a title, select the Gridlines tab and the "Major gridlines" for the x- and y-axes, and de-select "Show legend" from the "Legend" tab. Click Finish.

Move the cursor onto the chart, but not over the grid area or title--you should see "Chart Area"--and click to select the entire chart. You can drag the "handles" to resize the graph.

 

Showing multiples of pi on the x-axis

If you place the cursor near an x-axis number, you should see "Value (X) axis". Double-click and the "Format Axis" window should pop up. You can play with the Patterns options if you like, but click on the "Scale" tab and set what corresponds to Window settings on a TI graphics calculator. Click on the "Font" tab and change the font to Symbol. Next click on the "Number" tab and select the Category "Custom" at the bottom of the box on the left. In the "Type:" box on the right, type in "0.0p" and click on OK.

If you point to the curve, you should see something like 'Series 1 point "1.3" ' and perhaps two coordinates--double-click there and you can set options on the curve. Try setting a new color and weight--I rarely use other options--and click OK.

Now place the cursor on the plot area but not on the curve or axes labels. You should see "Plot Area." Double-click there, and on the right select "None" for the "Area." Click on OK.

Adding a second curve

Select cell C1 and type

=cos(pi()*a1)

and Enter. Select C1 again and double-click on the lower right corner of the cell. Select the entire graph with a click. Choose "Chart->Source data..." from the menubar. Click on the "Series" tab, then click on the Add button.

Click on the button at the right end of the X-Values box, select the data in column A (but not the whole column) and close the "Source Data - Values box. Click on the button at the right of the Y-values box, select the data in column C, and close the Source Data Values box. Click on OK. Point to the new curve, when you see something like 'Series 2 point "1.3" '--double-click there and set a new color. Click OK.

Labels and arrows

Check that you have the Drawing menu available: the menu usually begins with the word "Draw" followed by a pointer and then "Autoshapes." (If the menu is not visible, go the the menubar and click on View-->Toolbars--> and make sure that the "Drawing" box is checked.)

De-select the graph by clicking anywhere on the worksheet off the graph. Click on the Textbox icon (that looks like a small sheet of text with a capital "A" at the top, but do not select the slanted "A" for WordArt). Drag a small rectangle on your plot, and type "sin x". Drag the handles on the text box to fit the text snugly. Click on Format-->Text Box, and you can format the border (select the "Colors and Lines" tab) and the font and fill.

Create a similar text box for "cos x". Now click on the arrow icon, then drag an arrow from your "sin x" text box to the sine curve. Repeat with an arrow for the cosine label and graph. The arrows can also be formatted.

 

Parametric plots

For a typical graph of a function, you create only two columns of data, one for the x-values and one for the y-values (and you don't bother with the odd formatting to get pi on the x-axis). But for most parametric plots, you may first make an auxiliary column of t-values, then define x's and y's in terms of t. (Of course you might use different names for the variables.)

As a quick example, select the data you put in B1:C41 and create another "XY (Scatter)" plot: click on the chart icon, choose the "XY (Scatter)" chart type and the "Scatter with data points connected by smooth Lines without markers" Chart sub-type.

Click on Next, Next, and set gridlines and Legend as desired. Click on Finish, then adjust the size, plot area, and curve to your taste. You should get a unit circle (drawn twice, but once on top of the other). You may have to resize the graph to make the circle look like a circle.

 

In polar graphs, it is convenient to start with a few auxiliary columns before creating the x's and y's.

Polar Plots

Open the Excel file polar.xls to see samples. I have one column of values for theta, one column for values of r (defined as a function of theta), one column for x's and one column for y's. But I hid those columns by making the font white.

To create your own polar plot of the 3-petaled rose r = sin(3 theta), insert a new worksheet or open a new file. In A1 type "0" and Enter, and in A2 type "0.1" and Enter. Select both cells, then drag the black cross down until you have "6.3" in cell A64. In cell B1 type

=sin(3*a1)

and press Enter. Select B1 again and double-click on the lower right corner of the cell to perform a black cross copy.

Select C1 and type

=b1*cos(a1)

and press Enter. Perform another black cross copy in column C. Next select cell D1 and type

=b1*sin(a1)

and press Enter. Perform one more black cross copy in column D.

Select the data in columns C and D by dragging, then click on the chart icon. Choose the "XY (Scatter)" chart type and the "Scatter with data points connected by smooth Lines without markers" Chart sub-type.

Click on Next, Next, and set gridlines and Legend as desired. Click on Finish, then adjust the size, plot area, and curve to your taste. We'll come back to this graph below.

 

"Naming" functions and variables

Another potentially useful (but unnecessary) trick is to define "names" to represent variables and/or functions. The actual purpose of this Excel feature is to make it easier to understand the intent of some formula, analogous to the programming practice of giving variables descriptive names. For example, you might associate a name, such as "FudgeFactor," with a parameter stored in cell B1 that you use throughout your worksheet(s). Although an absolute cell reference (like "$B$1") could also be used, seeing the name can help you or another user to understand the significance of the value.

Go back to the polar.xls file and select the Butterfly. Click on Insert->Name->Define... and then click on the line with just the letter "f". In the box "Refers to:" delete everything except the "=COS(5*T)" and press OK. The plot should change to the 5-petaled rose r = cos(5 theta).

Now return to the polar graph (3-petaled rose) you made before. Select the cell B1. Click on Insert->Name->Define.., then type in "theta" and change the "Refers to:" box to read "= a1". Click OK. (You could have started with any cell not in the first column and simply make a relative reference to the cell on the left.)

Again click on Insert->Name->Define.., but this time type "f" and change the "Refers to:" box to read "1+cos(theta)" and click OK.

Now (while B1 is still selected) type "=f" and Enter. Select B1 again and double-click in its lower right corner. Your plot should now show the polar graph r = 1+cos(theta).

 

Some tricks

In the polar.xls file, I used a white font so that the values defining the plot are not visible. You can read about other tricks by clicking on the "technical issues" tab at the bottom of the Excel file.

 

Exporting graphs

You can copy any Excel graph and paste it into any Windows document. Click on the chart area to select the entire graph, and press CTRL+C (or click on Edit and Copy) to copy the graph. Open Word (or Paint, or ...) and press CTRL+V (or click on Edit and Paste) to paste the graph.
