  1. A mathematical model is a simplified description of reality that helps us understand a system or process.

  2. We can describe a relationship between variables with a table of values, a graph, or an equation.

  3. Linear models have equations of the form:

    \begin{gather*} y = (\blert{\text{starting value}}) + (\blert{\text{rate of change}}) \cdot x \end{gather*}

  4. The general form for a linear equation is: \(Ax + By = C\text{.}\)

  5. We can use the intercepts to graph a line. The intercepts are also useful for interpreting a model.

  6. The graph of an equation in two variables is just a picture of all its solutions.

  7. Lines have constant slope.

  8. The slope of a line gives us the rate of change of one variable with respect to another.

  9. Formulas for Linear Models

    • slope: \(\qquad\qquad m=\dfrac{\Delta y}{\Delta x} = \dfrac{Y_2-y_1}{x_2-x_1} \text{,}\) \(x_1 \ne x_2\)

    • slope-intercept form: \(\qquad\qquad y=b+mx \)

    • point-slopet form: \(\qquad\qquad y=y_1+m(x-x_1) \)

  10. The slope-intercept form is useful when we know the initial value and the rate of change.

  11. The point-slope form is useful when we know the rate of change and one point on the line.
