  • The graph of

    \begin{equation*} y = x^2 - 4x + 3 \end{equation*}

    has two \(x\)-intercepts, because the equation

    \begin{equation*} x^2 - 4x + 3 = 0 \end{equation*}

    has two real-valued solutions, \(x = 1\) and \(x = 3\text{.}\)

  • The graph of

    \begin{equation*} y = x^2 - 4x + 4 \end{equation*}

    has only one \(x\)-intercept, because the equation

    \begin{equation*} x^2 - 4x + 4 = 0 \end{equation*}

    has only one (repeated) real-valued solution, \(x = 2\text{.}\)

  • The graph of

    \begin{equation*} y = x^2 - 4x + 6 \end{equation*}

    has no \(x\)-intercepts, because the equation

    \begin{equation*} x^2 - 4x + 6 = 0 \end{equation*}

    has no real-valued solutions.
