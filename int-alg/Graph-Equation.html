Section 1.3 Graphs and Equations

Subsection Equations and Solutions

Definition 1.29. Solution.

A solution of an equation is a value of the variable that makes the equationtrue.

For example, \(x = −2\) is a solution of the equation

\begin{gather*} 4x^3 + 5x^2 -8x = 4 \end{gather*}

because \(4(-2)^3 + 5(-2)^2 - 8(-2) = -2 + 20 + 16 = 4\text{.}\)

Notebook 1.30. QuickCheck 1.

A linear equation has at most one solution. We find the solution by transforming the equation into a simpler equivalent equation whose solution is obvious.

Example 1.31.

Solve the equation \(3(2x-5)-4x=2x-(6-3x) \)

Solution

We begin by simplifying each side of the equation.

\begin{align*} 3(2x-5)-4x\amp =2x-(6-3x)\amp\amp\glert{\text{Apply the distrubutive law. }} \\ 6x-15-4x\amp =2x-6+3x\amp\amp\glert{\text{Combine like terms. }} \\ 2x-15\amp =5x-6\amp\amp\glert{\text{Add } {-5x+15} \text{ to both sides} } \\ -3x \amp = 9 \amp\amp \glert{\text{Divide both sides by }{-3.}} \\ x\amp = -3 \end{align*}

The solution is \(-3\text{.}\) You can check that substituting \(x=-3\) into the original equation produces a true statement.

Example 1.33.

Use algebra to solve the inequality \(5 -2x \lt 2\text{.}\)

Solution

We begin by isolating the term containing the variable, just as we dowhen solving a linear equation. We subtract 5 from both sides to obtain

\begin{gather*} -2x \lt -3 \end{gather*}

Then we divide both sides by −2 to find

\begin{align*} x\gt \frac{-3}{-2}\amp =\frac{3}{2}\amp\amp\glert{\text{Reverse the direction of the inequality.}} \end{align*}

Any value of \(x\) greater than \(\frac{3}{2} \) is a solution of the inequality. Because we cannot list all of these solutions, we often illustrate them as a graph on a number line, as shown below.

In the Example above, we used the following rule for solving linear inequalities.

If we multiply or divide both sides of an inequality by a negative number, wemust reverse the direction of the inequality.

Other than the rule stated in the box above, the rules for solving a linear inequality are the same as the rules for solving a linear equation.

Subsection Equations in Two Variables

An equation in two variables, such as

\begin{gather*} -3x+4y=24 \end{gather*}

has many solutions. Each solution consists of an ordered pair of values, one for \(x\) and one for \(y\text{,}\) that together satisfy the equation (make the equation true.)

Notebook 1.36. QuickCheck 3.
Example 1.37.

  1. Is \((2,7.5) \) a solution of the equation \(-3x+4y=24\text{?}\)

  2. Is \((4,3) \) a solution of the equation \(-3x+4y=24\text{?}\)

Solution

  1. The ordered pair \((2,7.5) \) is a solution of the equation above, because it satifies the equation.

    \begin{gather*} -3(\alert{2})+4(\alert{7.5})=-6+30=24 \end{gather*}

  2. The ordered pair \((4,3) \) is not a solution, because it does not satify the equation.

    \begin{gather*} -3(\alert{4})+4(\alert{3})=-12+12=0\ne 24 \end{gather*}

Subsection What is the Graph of an Equation?

Definition 1.39. Graph.

The graph of an equation in two variables is just a picture of all its solutions.

You might think it would be difficult to find all the solutions of an equation, but for a linear equation \(Ax + By = C\text{,}\) we can at least illustrate the solutions: all the solutions lie on a straight line. (Later on we can prove that this is true.)

Notebook 1.40. QuickCheck 4.
Example 1.41.

The figure shows a graph of the equation \(Ax + By = C\text{.}\) Which of the following equations are true?

  1. \(24B=C\)

  2. \(Ap+Bq=C\)

  3. \(Ag+Bh=C\)

Solution

  1. The point \((0, 24)\) lies on the graph, so \(x = 0\text{,}\) \(y = 24\) is a solution of the equation. Thus, \(A \cdot 0 + B \cdot 24 = C\text{,}\) or \(24B = C\) is a true statement.

  2. The point \((p, q)\) does not lie on the graph, so \(x = p\text{,}\) \(y = q\) does not satisfy the equation, and \(A p+ B q = C\) is a not true.

  3. The point \((g,h )\) does lie on the graph, so \(x = g\text{,}\) \(y = h\) does satisfy the equation, and \(A g+ B h = C\text{,}\) is a true.

Subsection Graphical Solution of Equations

We can use graphs to find solutions to equations in one variable.

Example 1.43.

Use the graph of \(y = 285 - 15x\) to solve the equation \(150 = 285 - 15x\text{.}\)

Solution

Compare the two equations in the problem. In the equation we wan to solve, \(y\) has been replaced by 150. We begin by locating the point \(P\) on the graph for which \(y = 150\text{.}\) Next we find the \(x\)-coordinate of point \(P\) by drawing an imaginary line from \(P\) straight down to the \(x\)-axis. The \(x\)-coordinate of \(P\) is \(x = 9\text{.}\)

Thus, \(P\) is the point \((9,150)\text{,}\) and \(x = 9\) when \(y = 150\text{.}\) The solution we seek is \(x = 9\text{.}\)

You can verify the solution algebraically by substituting \(x = \alert{9}\) into the equation:

Does \(150 = 285 - 15(\alert{9})\text{?}\)

\begin{equation*} 285 - 15(\alert{9}) = 285 - 135 = 150. ~~~~~~\glert{\text{Yes}} \end{equation*}

The relationship between an equation and its graph is an important one. For the previous example, make sure you understand that the following three statement sare equivalent.

  1. The point \((9, 150)\) lies on the graph of \(y = 285 - 15x\text{.}\)

  2. The ordered pair \((9, 150)\) is a solution of the equation \(y = 285 - 15x\text{.}\)

  3. \(x=9\) is a solution of the equation \(150 = 285 - 15x\text{.}\)

Subsection Graphical Solution of Inequalities

We can also use graphs to solve inequalities. Consider the inequality

\begin{gather*} 285-15x \ge 150 \end{gather*}

To solve this inequality means finding all values of \(x\) that make the expression \(285 -15x\) greater than or equal to 150. We could begin by trying some values of \(x\text{.}\) Here is a table obtained by evaluating \(285 − 15x\text{.}\)

\(x\) \(0\) \(2\) \(4\) \(6\) \(8\) \(10\) \(12\)
\(285-15x\) \(285\) \(255\) \(225\) \(195\) \(165\) \(135\) \(105\)

From the table, we see that values of \(x\) less than or equal to 8 are solutions ofthe inequality, but we have not checked all possible \(x\)-values. We can get a more complete picture from a graph

Example 1.45.

Here is a graph of the equation \(y = 285 - 15x\text{.}\) Use the graph to solve the inequality

\begin{equation*} 285 - 15x \gt 150 \end{equation*}
Solution

We look for points on the graph with \(y\)-coordinates greater than or equal to 150. These points are shown in color. Which \(x\)-values produced these points with \(y\)-values greater or equal to 150?

We can read the \(x\)-coordinates by dropping straight down to the \(x\)-axis, as shown by the arows. For example, the \(x\)-value corresponding to \(285-15x=150\) is \(x=9\text{.}\) For larger values of \(285-15x\text{,}\) we must choose \(x\)-values less than 9. Thus the solutions are all values of \(x\) less than or equal to 9, as shown on the \(x\)-axis.

Subsection Using a Graphing Utility

We can use a graphing utility to graph equations if they are written in the form \(y = (\text{expression in }x)\text{.}\) On most calculators, we follow three steps.

To Graph an Equation.

  1. Press Y= and enter the equation you wish to graph.

  2. Press WINDOW and select a graphing window.

  3. Press GRAPH.

Choosing a graphing window corresponds to drawing the \(x\)- and \(y\)-axes and marking a scale on each axis when we graph by hand. The standard graphing window displays values from \(-10\) to \(10\) on both axes. We can press the ZOOM key and then the number \(6\) to select this window.

Example 1.47.

Use a graphing utility to graph the equation \(3x + 2y = 16\text{.}\)

Solution

First, we solve the equation for \(y\) in terms of \(x\text{.}\)

\begin{align*} 3x+2y \amp = 16\amp\amp\glert{\text{Subtract } 3x \text{ from both sides.}} \\ 2y\amp = -3x+16 \amp\amp\glert{\text{Divide both sides by 2.}} \\ y\amp = \frac{-3x}{2}+\frac{16}{2}\amp\amp \glert{\text{Simplify.}} \\ y\amp= -1.5x+8 \end{align*}

Now press Y= and enter \(-1.5x+8\) after \(Y_1=\text{.}\) (Use the negation key, (-), to enter \(-1.5x\text{;}\) do not use the subtraction key.) We choose the standard graphing window by pressing ZOOM 6. The graph is shown at right.

When you want to erase the graph, press Y= and then press CLEAR. This deletes the expression following \(Y_1\)

Subsection Finding Coordinates with a Graphing Calculator

We can use the TRACE feature to find the coordinates of points on a graph.

Example 1.49.

Use a graph to find a solution to the equation \(y = -2.6x - 5.4\) with \(y\)-coordinate \(-10.6\text{.}\)

Solution

First we graph the equation \(y = -2.6x - 5.4\) in the window

Xmin\(=-5\) Xmax\(=4.4\)
Ymin\(=-20\) Ymax\(=15\)

We press TRACE, and a "bug" begins flashing on the display. The coordinates of the bug appear at the bottom of the display, as shown in the figure. We use the left and right arrows to move the bug along the graph. You can check that the coordinates of the point \((2, -10.6)\) do satisfy the equation \(y = -2.6x - 5.4\text{.}\)
