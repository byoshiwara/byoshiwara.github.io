To prove that the graph in Example 1 is really a circle, we convert the equation \(r=2\sin \theta\) to Cartesian form. First, multiply both sides by \(r\) to obtain

\begin{equation*} r^2=2r\sin \theta \end{equation*}

Next, replace \(r^2\) by \(x^2+y^2\) and \(r\sin \theta\) by \(y\text{,}\) to get

\begin{equation*} x^2+y^2 = 2y \end{equation*}

This equation is quadratic in two variables, so its graph is a conic section. We put the equation in standard form by completing the square in \(y\text{.}\)

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}[t] x^2+y^2 - 2y \amp = 0 \amp \amp \blert{\text{Add 1 to both sides.}}\\ x^2+(y^2-2y \alert{+1}) \amp =\alert{1} \amp \amp \blert{\text{Write the equation in standard form.}}\\ (x-0)^2 + (y-1)^2 \amp = 1\\ \end{aligned} \end{equation*}

We have the equation of a circle with center \((0,1)\) and radius \(1\text{.}\)
