For Problems 32–33, use the graphs to answer the questions.

32.

Francine's new puppy weighed 3.8 pounds when she brought it home, and it should gain approximately 0.6 pound per week. The graph shows the puppy's weight, \(W\text{,}\) after \(t\) weeks.

  1. Estimate the puppy's weight after 8 weeks.
  2. Estimate how long it will take for the puppy to reach 26 pounds.
  3. Write an algebraic equation for \(W\) in terms of \(t\text{.}\)
  4. Write and solve an equation to verify your answer to part (b).
33.

Marvin's telephone company charges $13 a month plus $0.15 per minute. The graph shows Marvin's phone bill, \(P\text{,}\) if he talks for \(m\) minutes.

  1. Estimate Marvin's phone bill if he talks for 45 minutes.
  2. Estimate the number of minutes Marvin talked if his phone bill is $25.
  3. Write an algebraic equation for \(P\) in terms of \(m\text{.}\)
  4. Write and solve an equation to verify your answer to part (b).
in-context ======= Exercise Group 33–38.

Problems 33–38 consider the ambiguous case of the Law of Sines, when two sides and an angle opposite one of them are known.

33.

In the right triangle \(ABC\) shown, \(\angle A = 30\degree,~\angle C = 90\degree\text{,}\) and \(c = 3\) inches.

  1. Use the definition of \(\sin A\) to solve for \(a\) (the length of side \(\overline{BC}\)).
  2. Can you draw a triangle \(ABC\) with \(A = 30\degree\) and \(c = 3\) if \(a \lt \dfrac{3}{2}\text{?}\) Why or why not?
  3. How many triangles are possible if \(\dfrac{3}{2} \lt a \lt 3\text{?}\)
  4. How many triangles are possible if \(a \gt 3\text{?}\)
34.

In this problem we show that there are two different triangles \(ABC\) with \(A = 30\degree,~ a = 2\) and \(c = 3\text{.}\)

  1. Use a protractor to draw an angle \(A = 30\degree\text{.}\) Mark point \(B\) on one side of the angle so that \(\overline{AB}\) is 3 inches long.
  2. Locate two distinct points on the other side of the angle that are each 2 inches from point \(B\text{.}\) These points are both possible locations for point \(C\text{.}\)
  3. Use the Law of Sines to find two distinct possible measures for \(\angle C\text{.}\)
35.

In \(\triangle ABC, \angle A = 30\degree\) and \(c = 12\text{.}\) How many triangles are possible for each of the following lengths for side \(a\text{?}\) Sketch the solutions in each case.

  1. \(\displaystyle a = 6\)
  2. \(\displaystyle a = 4\)
  3. \(\displaystyle a = 9\)
  4. \(\displaystyle a = 15\)
36.

Consider the triangle \(ABC\) shown below.

  1. Express the length of the altitude in terms of \(\angle A\) and \(c\text{.}\)
  2. Now suppose we keep \(\angle A\) and side \(c\) fixed, but allow \(a\) to vary in length. What is the smallest value \(a\) can have and still be long enough to make a triangle?
  3. What are the largest and smallest values that \(a\) can have in order to produce two distinct triangles \(ABC\) (without changing \(\angle A\) and side \(c\))?
