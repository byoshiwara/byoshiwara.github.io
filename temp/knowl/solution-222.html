Solution 10.68.1.

We use the polar form, \(z= 2\left(\cos \dfrac{\pi}{6} + i\sin \dfrac{\pi}{6}\right)\text{,}\) and apply De Moivre's theorem. Then

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}[t] z^4 \amp = 2^4\left(\cos 4 \cdot \dfrac{\pi}{6}+i\sin 4 \cdot \dfrac{\pi}{6}\right)\\ \amp=16\left(\cos \dfrac{2\pi}{3} + i\sin \dfrac{2\pi}{3}\right)\\ \end{aligned} \end{equation*}

The Cartesian form of \(z^4\) is thus \(16\left(\dfrac{-1}{2} + i\dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2}\right)\text{,}\) or \(-8+8i\sqrt{3}\text{.}\) You can check that you get this same result if you compute \(\left(\sqrt{3} + i\right)^4\) by expanding the power.
