in-context ======= Solution 8.4.1.

We can write \(105\degree\) as the sum of two special angles: \(105\degree = 60\degree + 45\degree\text{.}\) Now apply the addition formula for cosine.

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}[t] \cos (60\degree + 45\degree) \amp = \cos 60\degree \cos 45\degree - \sin 60\degree \sin 45\degree\\ \amp = \dfrac{1}{2} \dfrac{\sqrt{2}}{2}-\dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2} \dfrac{\sqrt{2}}{2} = \dfrac{\sqrt{2}-\sqrt{6}}{4}\\ \end{aligned} \end{equation*}

Thus, \(\cos 105\degree = \dfrac{\sqrt{2}-\sqrt{6}}{4}\text{.}\) You can check that your calculator gives the same decimal approximation of about \(-0.2588\) for both \(\cos 105\degree\) and \(\dfrac{\sqrt{2}-\sqrt{6}}{4}\text{.}\)
