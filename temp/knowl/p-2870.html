A linear equation written in the form

\begin{equation*} \blert{y=mx+b} \end{equation*}

is said to be in slope-intercept form. The coefficient \(m\) is the slope of the graph, and\(b\) is the \(y\)-intercept.

in-context =======

We begin by isolating \(\sin \beta\text{.}\)

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}[t] \dfrac{\sin \beta}{5} - 3 \amp = 1 \amp \amp \blert{\text{Add 3 to both sides.}}\\ \dfrac{\sin \beta}{5} \amp = 4 \amp \amp \blert{\text{Multiply both sides by 5.}}\\ \sin \beta \amp = 20\\ \end{aligned} \end{equation*}

Because \(\sin \beta\) is never greater than 1, there is no angle \(\beta\) whose sine is 20. The equation has no solution.
