    Section 2.1 Nonlinear Models

    In Chapter 1, we considered models described by linear functions. In this chapter, we begin our study of nonlinear models.

    Subsection Solving Nonlinear Equations

    When studying nonlinear models, we will need to solve nonlinear equations. For example, in Investigation 2 we used a graph to solve the quadratic equation
    \begin{equation*} 18x - x^2 = 80 \end{equation*}
    Here is another example. The figure shows a table and a graph for the function \(y = 2x^2 - 5\text{.}\)
    \(x\) \(-3\) \(-2\) \(-1\) \(0\) \(1\) \(2\) \(3\)
    \(y\) \(13\) \(3\) \(-3\) \(-5\) \(-3\) \(3\) \(13\)
    You can see that there are two points on the graph for each \(y\)-value greater than \(-5\text{.}\) For example, the two points with \(y\)-coordinate \(7\) are shown. To solve the equation
    \begin{equation*} 2x^2 - 5 = 7 \end{equation*}
    we need only find the \(x\)-coordinates of these points. From the graph, the solutions appear to be about \(2.5\) and \(-2.5\text{.}\)
    How can we solve this equation algebraically? The opposite operation for squaring a number is taking a square root. So we can undo the operation of squaring by extracting square roots. We first solve for \(x^2\) to get
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}[t] 2x^2 \amp= 12 \\ x^2 \amp= 6\\ \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    and then take square roots to find
    \begin{equation*} x = \pm\sqrt{6} \end{equation*}

    Caution 2.1.

    Don’t forget that every positive number has two square roots. The symbol \(\blert{\pm}\) (read “plus or minus”) is a shorthand notation used to indicate both square roots of \(6\text{.}\)
    The exact solutions are thus \(\sqrt{6}\) and \(-\sqrt{6}\text{.}\) We can also find decimal approximations for the solutions using a calculator. Rounded to two decimal places, the approximate solutions are \(2.45\) and \(-2.45\text{.}\)

    Checkpoint 2.2. QuickCheck 1.

    Which statement is true?
    • A quadratic equation may include a linear or a constant term.
    • The solutions of a quadratic equation are always of the form \(\pm k\) .
    • Your calculator gives exact decimal values for square roots of integers.
    • The coefficients of a quadratic equation are called parabolas.
    Answer.
    \(\text{Choice 1}\)
    Solution.
    A quadratic equation may include a linear or a constant term.
    In general, we can solve equations of the form \(~ax^2 + c = 0~\) by isolating \(x^2\) on one side of the equation and then taking the square root of each side. This method for solving equations is called extraction of roots.

    Extraction of Roots.

    To solve the equation
    \begin{equation*} ax^2 + c = 0 \end{equation*}
    1. Isolate \(x^2\text{.}\)
    2. Take square roots of both sides. There are two solutions.

    Example 2.3.

    If a cat falls off a tree branch 20 feet above the ground, its height \(t\) seconds later is given by \(h = 20 - 16t^2\text{.}\)
    1. What is the height of the cat \(0.5\) second later?
    2. How long does the cat have to get in position to land on its feet before it reaches the ground?
    Solution.
    1. In this question, we are given a value of \(t\) and asked to find the corresponding value of \(h\text{.}\) To do this, we evaluate the formula for \(t = 0.5\text{.}\) We substitute \(\alert{0.5}\) for \(t\) into the formula and simplify.
      \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}[t] h \amp= 20 - 16(\alert{0.5})^2 \amp\amp \blert{\text{Compute the power.}}\\ \amp = 20 - 16 (0.25) \amp\amp \blert{\text{Multiply; then subtract.}}\\ \amp = 20 - 4 = 16 \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
      The cat is \(16\) feet above the ground after \(0.5\) second.
    2. We would like to find the value of \(t\) when the height, \(h\text{,}\) is known. We substitute \(h = \alert{0}\) into the equation to obtain
      \begin{equation*} \alert{0} = 20 - 16t^2 \end{equation*}
      To solve this equation, we use extraction of roots. First we isolate \(t^2\) on one side of the equation.
      \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}[t] 16t^2 \amp= 20 \amp\amp \blert{\text{Divide by 16.}}\\ t^2 \amp= \frac{20}{16}=1.25 \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
      Then we take the square root of both sides of the equation to find
    \begin{equation*} t = \pm \sqrt{1.25} \approx \pm1.118 \end{equation*}
    Only the positive solution makes sense here, so the cat has approximately 1.12 seconds to get into position for landing.

    Subsection Solving Formulas

    We can use extraction of roots to solve many formulas involving the square of the variable.

    Example 2.8.

    The formula
    \begin{equation*} V = \dfrac{1}{3} \pi r^2h \end{equation*}
    gives the volume of a cone in terms of its height and radius. Solve the formula for \(r\) in terms of \(V\) and \(h\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    Because the variable we want is squared, we use extraction of roots. First, we multiply both sides by \(3\) to clear the fraction.
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}[t] \alert{3}V \amp = \alert{3}\left(\frac{1}{3} \pi r^2h\right)\\ 3V \amp = \pi r^2h \amp\amp \blert{\text{Divide both sides by } \pi h.}\\ \frac{3V}{\pi h}\amp= r^2 \amp\amp \blert{\text{Take square roots.}}\\ \pm\sqrt{\dfrac{3V}{\pi h}}\amp= r \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    Because the radius of a cone must be a positive number, we use only the positive square root: \(~~r = \sqrt{\dfrac{3V}{\pi h}}\text{.}\)

    Subsection More Extraction of Roots

    Equations of the form
    \begin{equation*} \blert{a(px + q)^2 + r = 0} \end{equation*}
    can also be solved by extraction of roots after isolating the squared expression, \((px + q)^2\text{.}\)

    Example 2.10.

    Solve the equation \(~3(x - 2)^2 = 48\)
    Solution.
    First, we isolate the perfect square, \((x - 2)^2\text{.}\)
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}[t] 3(x - 2)^2 \amp = 48 \amp\amp\blert{\text{Divide both sides by }3.}\\ (x - 2)^2 \amp = 16 \amp\amp\blert{\text{Take the square root of each side.}}\\ x - 2 \amp = \pm\sqrt{16} = \pm 4 \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    This gives us two equations for \(x\text{,}\)
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}[t] x - 2 \amp =4 \amp\amp \text{or }\amp x - 2 \amp =-4\amp\amp\blert{\text {Solve each equation.}}\\ x \amp=6 \amp\amp \text{or }\amp x \amp =-2 \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    The solutions are \(6\) and \(-2\text{.}\)
    Here is a general strategy for solving equations by extraction of roots.

    Extraction of Roots.

    To solve the equation
    \begin{equation*} a(px+q)^2 +r=0 \end{equation*}
    1. Isolate the squared expression, \((px + q)^2\text{.}\)
    2. Take the square root of each side of the equation. Remember that a positive number has two square roots.
    3. Solve each equation. There are two solutions.

    Subsection Compound Interest and Inflation

    Many savings institutions offer accounts on which the interest is compounded annually. At the end of each year, the interest earned is added to the principal, and the interest for the next year is computed on this larger sum of money.

    Compound Interest.

    If interest is compounded annually for \(n\) years, the amount, \(A\text{,}\) of money in an account is given by
    \begin{equation*} A = P(1 + r)^n \end{equation*}
    where \(P\) is the principal and \(r\) is the interest rate, expressed as a decimal fraction.

    Example 2.13.

    Carmella invests $3000 in an account that pays an interest rate, \(r\text{,}\) compounded annually.
    1. Write an expression for the amount of money in Carmella’s account after two years.
    2. What interest rate would be necessary for Carmella’s account to grow to $3500 in two years?
    Solution.
    1. We use the formula above with \(P = 3000\) and \(n = 2\text{.}\) Carmella’s account balance will be
      \begin{equation*} A = 3000(1 + r)^2 \end{equation*}
    2. We substitute \(3500\) for \(A\) in the equation.
      \begin{equation*} \alert{3500} = 3000(1 + r)^2 \end{equation*}
      We can solve this equation in \(r\) by extraction of roots. First, we isolate the perfect square.
      \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}[t] 3500 \amp = 3000(1 + r )^2 \amp\amp \blert{\text{Divide both sides by 3000.}} \\ 1.1\overline{6} \amp = (1 + r )^2 \amp\amp \blert{\text{Take the square root of both sides.}} \\ \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
      This give us two equations to solve.
      \begin{align*} \pm 1.0801 \amp\approx 1 + r \amp\amp \blert{\text{Subtract 1 from both sides.}}\\ r \approx 0.0801 \text{ or } r \amp \approx -2.0801 \end{align*}
      Because the interest rate must be a positive number, we discard the negative solution. Carmella needs an account with interest rate \(r \approx 0.0801\text{,}\) or just over 8%, to achieve an account balance of $3500 in two years.

    The formula for compound interest also applies to the effects of inflation. For instance, if there is a steady inflation rate of 4% per year, in two years an item that now costs $100 will cost
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}[t] A \amp= P(1 + r)^2\\ \amp = 100(1 + 0.04)^2 = \$108.16 \end{aligned} \end{equation*}

    Subsection Other Nonlinear Equations

    Because squaring and taking square roots are opposite operations, we can solve the equation
    \begin{equation*} \sqrt{x} = 8.2 \end{equation*}
    by squaring both sides to get
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}[t] (\sqrt{x})^2 \amp= 8.2^2 \\ x \amp = 67.24 \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    Similarly, we can solve
    \begin{equation*} x^3 = 258 \end{equation*}
    by taking the cube root of both sides, because cubing and taking cube roots are opposite operations. Rounding to three places, we find
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}[t] \sqrt[3]{x^3}\amp=258\\ x \amp \approx 6.366 \end{aligned} \end{equation*}

    The notion of undoing operations can help us solve a variety of simple nonlinear equations. The operation of taking a reciprocal is its own opposite, so we solve the equation
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{x} = 50 \end{equation*}
    by taking the reciprocal of both sides to get
    \begin{equation*} x = \frac{1}{50}= 0.02 \end{equation*}

    Example 2.17.

    Solve \(~~\dfrac{3}{x - 2}= 4\)
    Solution.
    We begin by taking the reciprocal of both sides of the equation to get
    \begin{equation*} \frac{x - 2}{3}= \frac{1}{4} \end{equation*}
    We continue to undo the operations in reverse order. First, we multiply both sides by \(3\text{.}\)
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}[t] x - 2 \amp = \frac{3}{4} \amp\amp \blert{\text{Add 2 to both sides.}}\\ x \amp = 2 + \frac{3}{4}= \frac{11}{4} \amp\amp \blert{\tfrac{2}{1}+ \tfrac{3}{4}=\tfrac{8}{4}+\tfrac{3}{4}=\tfrac{11}{4}}\\ \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    The solution is \(\dfrac{11}{4}\text{,}\) or \(2.75\text{.}\)
