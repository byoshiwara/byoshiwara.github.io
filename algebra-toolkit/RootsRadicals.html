Section 3.3 Section 3.3 Roots and Radicals

Subsection 1. Use the definition of root

Subsubsection Examples

Example 3.29.

Simplify. Do not use a calculator!

  1. \(\displaystyle (\sqrt{7})(\sqrt{7})\)
  2. \(\displaystyle \sqrt{n}(\sqrt{n})\)
Solution

By the definition of square root, \(\sqrt{a}\) is a number whose square is \(a\text{.}\)

  1. \(\displaystyle (\sqrt{7})(\sqrt{7})=7\)
  2. \(\displaystyle \sqrt{n}(\sqrt{n})=n\)
Example 3.30.

Simplify. Do not use a calculator!

  1. \(\displaystyle (\sqrt[3]{5})^3\)
  2. \(\displaystyle (\sqrt[3]{4})(\sqrt[3]{4})(\sqrt[3]{4})\)
Solution

By the definition of square root, \(\sqrt[3]{a}\) is a number whose cube is \(a\text{.}\)

  1. \(\displaystyle (\sqrt[3]{5})^3=5\)
  2. \(\displaystyle (\sqrt[3]{4})(\sqrt[3]{4})(\sqrt[3]{4})=4\)
Example 3.31.

Simplify. Do not use a calculator!

  1. \(\displaystyle \dfrac{3}{\sqrt{3}}\)
  2. \(\displaystyle (\sqrt{13})^2\)
Solution
  1. \(\displaystyle \dfrac{3}{\sqrt{3}} = \dfrac{\sqrt{3}\sqrt{3}}{\sqrt{3}} = \sqrt{3}\)
  2. \(\displaystyle (\sqrt{13})^2 = 13\)

Subsection 2. Approximate rational numbers

Subsubsection Examples

Example 3.35.

Identify each number as rational or irrational.

  1. \(\displaystyle \sqrt{6}\)
  2. \(\displaystyle \dfrac{-5}{3}\)
  3. \(\displaystyle \sqrt{16}\)
  4. \(\displaystyle \sqrt{\dfrac{5}{9}}\)
Solution
  1. Irrational: \(~\sqrt{6}~\) is not the quotient of two integers.

  2. Rational: \(~\dfrac{-5}{3}~\) is the quotient of two integers.

  3. Rational: \(~\sqrt{16} = 4~\) is an integer.

  4. Irrational: \(~\sqrt{\dfrac{5}{9}} = \dfrac{\sqrt{5}}{3}~\text{,}\) but \(\sqrt{5}\) is irrational.

Example 3.36.

Give a decimal approximation rounded to thousandths.

  1. \(\displaystyle 5\sqrt{3}\)
  2. \(\displaystyle \dfrac{-2}{3}\sqrt{21}\)
  3. \(\displaystyle 2+\sqrt[3]{5}\)
Solution

Use a calculator to evaluate.

  1. Enter \(~5~ \boxed{\sqrt{}}~\) and round to three decimal places: \(~8.660 \)
  2. Enter \(~\boxed{(-)}~2~\boxed{\sqrt{}}~21~\boxed{)}~\boxed{\div}~3~\) and round to three decimal places: \(~-3.055\)
  3. Enter \(~2~\boxed{(+)}~\boxed{\text{MATH}}~\boxed{4}~5~\) and round to three decimal places: \(~3.710\)

Subsection 3. Use the order of operations

Subsubsection Examples

Example 3.39.

Simplify each expression. Do not use a calculator!

  1. \(\displaystyle \dfrac{4-\sqrt{64}}{2}\)
  2. \(\displaystyle -2(3\sqrt{16}-\sqrt{3(27)})\)
  3. \(\displaystyle 6-3\sqrt[3]{27-7(5)}\)
Solution
  1. \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}[t] \dfrac{4-\sqrt{64}}{2} \amp = \dfrac{4-8}{2} \amp\amp \blert{\text{Evaluate the radical.}}\\ \amp = \dfrac{-4}{2}=-2 \amp\amp \blert{\text{Simplify the numerator.}} \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
  2. \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}[t] -2(3\sqrt{16}-\sqrt{3(27)}) \amp = -2(3 \cdot 4-\sqrt{81}) \amp\amp \blert{\text{Evaluate the radicals.}}\\ \amp = -2(12-9)=-6 \amp\amp \blert{\text{Simplify inside the parentheses.}} \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
  3. \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}[t] 6-3\sqrt[3]{27-7(5)} \amp = 6-3\sqrt[3]{27-35} \amp\amp \blert{\text{Simplify under the radical.}}\\ \amp = 6-3\sqrt[3]{-8}=6-3(-2)=12 \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
Example 3.40.

Simplify each expression. Round your answer to hundredths.

  1. \(\displaystyle \dfrac{8-2\sqrt{2}}{4}\)
  2. \(\displaystyle 2+6\sqrt[3]{-25}\)
Solution

  1. Enter

    \begin{equation*} \boxed{(}~8~\boxed{-}~2~\boxed{\sqrt{}}~\boxed{)}~\boxed{)}~\boxed{\div}~4 \end{equation*}

    and round to two decimal places: \(~1.29\)

  2. Enter

    \begin{equation*} ~2~\boxed{+}~6~\boxed{\text{MATH}}~\boxed{4}~\boxed{-}~25 \end{equation*}

    and round to two decimal places: \(~-15.54\)
